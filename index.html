If your number felt bigger than you expected — that's normal. Most families are.

The gap between what care costs and what's available isn't a failure of planning. It's a reflection of a system that wasn't designed to make this easy. You're not behind. You just now have a real number to work with instead of a worry with no shape.

What to do with this estimate

If you have a gap: The earlier your family sees this number, the more options you have. Long-term care insurance, asset planning, family contribution conversations, and Medicaid planning all take time. The families who navigate this best are the ones who started the conversation before they had to.

If you have a surplus: That's good news — and it's worth protecting. Knowing your coverage now means you can make decisions from a position of strength rather than scrambling when circumstances change.

Either way: This number belongs in a family conversation. Print it. Save it. Bring it to a sibling meeting. The worst thing you can do with it is close this tab and go back to not knowing.

You're not selfish for thinking about the financial impact on your family. You're being honest about something most people are afraid to say out loud. The cost of caring for a parent is real. The worry it creates is real. And facing it — even when the number is hard — is an act of love, not a betrayal of it.

What families usually do next

  • Have the first conversation with your parent about their wishes
  • Locate legal documents — POA, will, advance directive
  • Review insurance policies and coverage gaps
  • Estimate what family contribution is realistic
  • Create an emergency contact and medical information sheet
  • Start a caregiving binder
